The problem

EHS teams re-type the same incident five times.

An injury at a manufacturing site today travels through:

  1. A worker form
  2. A supervisor's spreadsheet
  3. An internal investigation tracker
  4. A regulator portal (OSHA ITA, HSE, Notify NSW)
  5. The corporate KPI deck
Each hop loses fidelity, adds latency, and breaks the chain of custody an inspector cares about.

When the inspector arrives and asks "show me what you reported and when" — the answer is in five different places, none of them signed, none of them tamper-evident.

Deep dive — OSHA Form 300A

Certify once. Frozen forever.

When an EHS lead certifies the annual 300A summary, Safelync writes the column totals into an immutable snapshot table.

  • Live aggregation feeds the draft view (red DRAFT stamp).
  • Certified version reads the snapshot (green CERTIFIED badge).
  • Post-certification mutation cannot drift the signed document.
  • Verbatim 1904.32(b)(3) affirmation embedded in the PDF.
  • Certifier-title validated against the 4-key regulatory allowlist.
This is exactly what 29 CFR 1904.32(b)(5) requires.

Most competitors regenerate the 300A from live data every time it's opened — and quietly mutate the historical record when an underlying incident is edited. Safelync doesn't.

Deadlines — the countdown nobody else has.

A unified deadline aggregator rolls up every applicable clock per incident:

US OSHA 1904.39

  • 8h — fatality
  • 24h — hospitalisation, amputation, eye loss

UK RIDDOR

  • Without delay — fatality, Reg 7, 11(1)
  • 10 days — over-7-day injury
  • 15 days — diseases
  • 14 days — gas incidents

AU SafeWork NSW

  • Immediately — phone notification (s.38)
  • 48h after request — written notification (s.38(4)(b))

Most competitors get this one wrong. We anchor on the request, not the incident.

Every list row gets a most-urgent badge. Every detail page shows the full deadline stack with urgency colours. Inspectors stop asking "did you notify?" — the answer is on the screen.

What we built — 6 of 8

Override approval — the two-person rule.

When a worker contests "OSHA-recordable" or "RIDDOR-reportable", supervisors review in a dedicated Approvals queue.

  • Requester ≠ decider — enforced at the database trigger.
  • Self-approval forbidden. Last-admin lockout protected.
  • Decisions land in the activity log with field-level diffs.
  • Whitelist of overridable fields (no scope creep).
Why this matters.

OSHA recordability disputes are one of the most common audit findings. Safelync makes the contest visible, traceable, and impossible to forge.

What we built — 7 of 8

Audit log — tamper-evident by construction.

  • SHA-256 hash chain per organisation. Each entry hashes prev_hash || canonical row.
  • Append-only. DELETE blocked at the trigger level.
  • Immutable once hydrated. BEFORE UPDATE triggers stop rehashing.
  • Captures who, when, IP, user-agent, and a field-level diff of every change.
  • Export as CSV for inspector requests.
This is the slide they'll remember.

Every regulator audit eventually asks for the activity trail. Safelync hands over a verifiable chain — anyone can recompute the hashes and prove no row was edited after the fact.
